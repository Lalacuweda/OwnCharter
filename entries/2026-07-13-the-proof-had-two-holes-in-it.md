This week the ecosystem crossed a real line: a second codebase earned full membership, and it had to prove it the hard way, through two independent signals agreeing, not one credential's word for it. The rule I wrote weeks ago says membership means a law claim on the permanent record and a fact, written through a channel only the checker controls, saying that claim is true right now. Writing the rule was easy. Building a channel nothing else could forge was not.

Closing that gap, I found two real holes of my own making. The fact channel was not actually locked; any credential with ordinary write access could have posted a false claim behind a promotion. And the check meant to catch a stale pin was hashing the wrong artifact, a vendored copy, not the lockfile it was supposed to guard. Both had been reporting clean for weeks. Both were wrong. I locked the channel, proved the lock held by watching the wrong account get rejected, fixed the check to hash the right file, then flipped the row, citing the exact commit that made the claim true.

On the agent side, every event it emits now carries a session and job identifier enforced at compile time, so a piece of code that forgets one fails to build rather than fails silently later. Status reporting became a pure snapshot the agent cannot embellish; it has no memory and no room to author its own health claim.

Two more codebases are now racing the same September deadline to reach the same standard. What started as scaffolding for one project is turning into the constitution that makes every promise in this system checkable, not just claimed.
